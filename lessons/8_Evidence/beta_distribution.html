Probability distributions

Beta distribution

Thomas Bayes · Karl Pearson · 18th–19th century  ·  Bayesian inference, conjugate priors

01 · History

The Beta distribution has roots in the work of Thomas Bayes (1702–1761), whose posthumous essay introduced what we now call Bayes' theorem. Bayes implicitly used a uniform prior — equivalent to Beta(1,1) — over an unknown probability.

The distribution was formally studied and named by Karl Pearson in the late 19th century as part of his systematic classification of continuous probability distributions. It belongs to what Pearson called the Type I family, defined on a bounded interval.

Its central role in Bayesian statistics stems from a key property: it is the conjugate prior for the binomial likelihood, meaning the posterior after observing coin tosses is again a Beta distribution.

02 · Mathematical definition

The Beta distribution is defined for θ ∈ [0, 1] with shape parameters α, β > 0:

π(θ; α, β) = θα−1 (1−θ)β−1 / B(α, β)
B(α, β) = Γ(α)Γ(β) / Γ(α+β)   (normalising constant)

04 · Interpretation of parameters

Think of α and β as counts from an imaginary prior experiment: α pseudo-observations of heads, β of tails. The prior mean α/(α+β) is the fraction of heads in that imaginary experiment.

After observing k real heads in n tosses, the posterior is Beta(α+k, β+n−k). The parameters simply accumulate evidence — no integration needed. This is the power of conjugacy.

05 · Euler Beta function vs. Beta distribution

These are two distinct objects that share a name and a formula — one is a function in mathematical analysis, the other is a probability distribution.

The Euler Beta function B(α, β) was introduced by Leonhard Euler around 1730 while studying integrals of the form ∫₀¹ tα−1(1−t)β−1 dt. It arose in the context of interpolating factorials and generalising the binomial coefficient to real numbers. Its definition is:

B(α, β) = ∫₀¹ tα−1(1−t)β−1 dt = Γ(α)Γ(β) / Γ(α+β)

It is a special function — a real-valued function of two positive real numbers, with no randomness involved. Euler was interested in it purely as an object of analysis.

The Beta distribution came later, formalised by Karl Pearson in the 19th century as a probability distribution on [0,1]. Its PDF is exactly the integrand of the Beta function, divided by B(α, β) to ensure it integrates to 1:

π(θ; α, β) = θα−1(1−θ)β−1 / B(α, β)

The relationship is therefore direct: the Beta function is the normalising constant of the Beta distribution. Without it, the PDF would not integrate to 1 and could not represent a probability distribution.
